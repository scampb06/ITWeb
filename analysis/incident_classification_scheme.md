## 1. Prevalent attacks

Globally, the most prevalent hybrid campaigns are not dramatic cyberwar set-pieces but **repeatable combinations** of intrusion, impersonation, amplification, and narrative laundering. The most common patterns in our incident set are: hack-and-leak operations against political actors; spoofed sites and cloned media brands used to launder narratives; coordinated inauthentic behavior on mainstream platforms; symbolic defacements or website compromises to create psychological effect; and low-impact but high-visibility DDoS attacks claimed by hacktivists to signal momentum and intimidate defenders. Russia-linked actors remain the most visible protagonists in overt cyber-plus-influence campaigns, especially around elections and wartime narratives, while PRC-linked actors appear more often in espionage-plus-influence, North Korean actors in deceptive malware and financial lures, and Iran-linked actors in targeted cyber-enabled influence and intimidation.[^11_3][^11_4][^11_5][^11_1]

In Africa, the dominant pattern is still **cyber-enabled influence rather than deep intrusion**. The Africa cases in our corpus are led by coordinated inauthentic behavior, fake local personas, pseudo-media fronts, manipulated Facebook/X ecosystems, and harassment or adversarial-reporting campaigns that suppress opponents without needing sophisticated network compromises. Russia- and Wagner-linked ecosystems are the most visible foreign protagonists in CAR, Sudan, Libya, Mali, and Burkina Faso, while domestic state-linked or party-linked actors feature heavily in Uganda, Tanzania, Ethiopia, Zimbabwe, and South Africa.[^11_6]

In South Africa specifically, the evidence base points more strongly to **influence operations in digital political discourse** than to classic hack-and-leak or destructive cyber incidents linked to influence campaigns. Public reporting and research cited in our Africa file describe suspected Russian-linked attempts to shape election discourse, as well as domestic coordinated inauthentic behavior using fake pages, pseudo-fact-checking, and leak-style political content. More recent research by the Centre for Analytics and Behavioural Change and Murmur suggests Russian online influence reaches South African audiences through bridge accounts, diplomatic channels, and ecosystem-level amplification rather than a proven breach-first model.[^11_7][^11_6]

## 2. Main drivers

Globally, hybrid campaigns are driven by three overlapping logics. The first is **geopolitical**: states use cyber-plus-influence operations to weaken rivals, shape elections, fracture alliances, reduce support for Ukraine, pressure Taiwan, or improve negotiating leverage without crossing the threshold into overt armed conflict. The second is economic: cybercrime crews and state-tolerated actors have learned that breach claims, leak sites, reputational pressure, and public extortion are often more lucrative than quiet theft alone. The third is technical: attackers can now combine stolen credentials, commodity malware, cloud compromise, cheap bot infrastructure, generative AI content, and platform-native amplification at relatively low cost; Mandiant’s 2025 data also shows exploits remained the top initial vector in 2024 and stolen credentials rose to the second most common vector at 16 percent.[^11_4][^11_1][^11_3]

In Africa, geopolitical drivers center on competition for influence, legitimacy, and alignment, especially where outside powers seek to displace Western partners or present themselves as guarantors of sovereignty and security. Economic drivers include the low cost of influence operations relative to conventional power projection, plus the appeal of information manipulation in fragile or under-moderated media ecosystems. Technical drivers are often less about elite intrusion tradecraft and more about the effectiveness of fake personas, meme pages, compromised or imitated brands, WhatsApp-forwarding cultures, and platform moderation gaps in multilingual environments.[^11_6]

In South Africa, the drivers are tied to the country’s geopolitical salience, polarized politics, and high online visibility of domestic debate. South Africa matters symbolically as a G20 member, BRICS participant, and continental agenda-setter, so foreign actors have incentives to seed favorable narratives into local discourse. At the same time, the local information environment rewards outrage, factional identity, anti-establishment messaging, and geopolitical framing, creating fertile ground for foreign narratives that are then domestically repurposed by political communities on both the left and right.[^11_8][^11_7]

## 3. Targets

Globally, the most targeted sectors are **elections and democratic institutions**, media and information intermediaries, critical infrastructure, and high-trust brands. Our corpus repeatedly shows campaigns against political parties, parliaments, candidates, election systems, journalists, broadcasters, and ministries; critical infrastructure appears most often when cyber disruption is meant to produce fear, coercive signaling, or a story of state weakness rather than purely operational damage. Vulnerable communities such as diasporas, minorities, and protest movements are also targeted because they are both politically salient and digitally reachable.[^11_5][^11_3][^11_4]

In Africa, the most common targets are public opinion during elections or coups, protest movements, opposition leaders, journalists, and civic actors. Several incidents in the Africa file show efforts to manipulate perceptions of Russia or Wagner, discredit democratic protest, suppress civil society, or distort election discourse. Rather than disabling infrastructure, many African campaigns aim to colonize the narrative space around legitimacy, sovereignty, anti-colonial resentment, or “security partnership” messaging.[^11_6]

In South Africa, the main targets are political discourse, party reputations, media trust, and communities already predisposed to polarizing content. The Daily Maverick/CABC-Murmur reporting suggests Russian influence vectors reached South African political communities through bridge accounts and selective amplification, including audiences on both ends of the ideological spectrum. That matters because South Africa’s media and political discourse often influence wider regional narratives, meaning even a “soft” influence effort can have outsized downstream effect.[^11_8][^11_7]

## 4. Breach claims and narratives

Globally, state-linked actors and their proxies increasingly treat the **public story of the breach** as part of the attack surface. In classic hack-and-leak cases such as the DNC, Podesta, and Macron leaks, the theft itself mattered less than the timed release, selective framing, forged add-ons, and laundering of material through cutout personas, leak sites, fringe forums, and then mainstream media attention. In newer campaigns, symbolic defacements, fake media brands, cloned domains, and noisy breach claims can shape public opinion even when the underlying technical impact is modest, because they signal reach, embarrass victims, and fill the information vacuum before defenders can establish facts.[^11_3][^11_4]

In Africa, leak-site style operations are less prominent than **authenticity laundering** via fake local pages, pseudo-journalism, and covert amplification. Russia-linked campaigns in CAR, Sudan, Libya, Mali, and Burkina Faso did not need spectacular breaches to shape opinion; they used fabricated local voices, proxy pages, and sustained narrative repetition to normalize pro-Russian or anti-Western frames. In Tanzania and South Africa, fake pages and pseudo-fact-checking assets show how adversaries can mimic trusted civic functions to suppress opponents or inject “evidence-like” claims into political debate.[^11_6]

In South Africa, the practical lesson is that even absent a major attributed intrusion, narratives can be made to feel evidential through screenshots, “exposé” pages, pseudo-leaks, and coordinated amplification. The South African First-linked activity in our Africa file explicitly used fake and misleading pages, including pseudo-fact-checking and leak-style pages, to distort political discussion. Combined with bridge-account behavior and foreign diplomatic amplification, this creates an ecosystem where the performance of disclosure can matter almost as much as verified disclosure itself.[^11_7][^11_6]

## 5. Coalescence

Globally, “patriotic hacktivism,” disinformation-as-a-service, and state-backed cyber warfare are **not identical**, but they are increasingly entangled. Governments benefit from cutouts who can launch DDoS attacks, claim breaches, run Telegram channels, or spread screenshots while preserving deniability; meanwhile mercenary influence operators and cybercriminal crews can rent out services or repurpose state-friendly narratives for profit. Anonymous Sudan is a useful example in the broader ecosystem: it publicly posed as a Sudanese or pro-Muslim hacktivist movement while researchers and reporting increasingly described it as a Russian information operation using DDoS theatrics and identity framing to amplify political effect.[^11_9][^11_10]

In Africa, the convergence is visible above all in the **proxy model**. Russia-linked influence in CAR, Sudan, Libya, Mali, and Burkina Faso often moved through ostensibly local or semi-local personas, media fronts, activists, or aligned communities rather than overt Kremlin branding. This does not always amount to kinetic cyber warfare, but it does show how state interests, outsourced messaging, and platform-native manipulation can blend into one operating model.[^11_6]

In South Africa, the evidence suggests partial coalescence rather than full fusion. Foreign influence appears to travel through bridge accounts, sympathetic communities, and selective amplification, while domestic actors adopt similar tactics for their own agendas. The result is an environment in which foreign state narratives, domestic factional politics, and opportunistic information entrepreneurs can reinforce each other even without formal coordination.[^11_8][^11_7]

## 6. Responses and preparation

Globally, the response is moving toward **whole-of-society resilience**, but unevenly. Governments and regional bodies are improving election stress tests, public attribution, sanctions, takedowns, and information-sharing; the EU and ENISA explicitly frame FIMI and cybersecurity as overlapping threat spaces, and incident response is increasingly paired with strategic communications and prebunking. Security leaders should therefore prepare for hybrid campaigns as business risks, not just SOC events: map which assets could be turned into narrative weapons, rehearse breach-plus-rumor scenarios, align cyber, legal, comms, and executive teams, monitor for brand impersonation and fake domains, and pre-author crisis communications before an incident occurs.[^11_2][^11_1][^11_4]

In Africa, effective response depends on strengthening both technical defense and information integrity. Civil society, fact-checkers, election observers, and independent media often provide the early warning on manipulative narratives, while platforms and governments remain inconsistent in multilingual moderation and transparency. For security leaders, that means building external relationships before a crisis, including with fact-checkers, telecoms, journalists, and sector peers, and not assuming that cyber defense alone will neutralize narrative exploitation.[^11_6]

In South Africa, the most practical preparation is to assume that any visible cyber incident may be **immediately politicized**. Organizations should treat stakeholder trust as a protected asset, establish rapid fusion cells linking cyber response with comms and public affairs, monitor open platforms for impersonation and narrative spikes, and exercise “day two” scenarios where a minor outage, data theft, or even false breach claim becomes a reputational campaign. That is especially important in South Africa’s banking, public-sector, telecoms, mining, and retail-heavy security community, which also aligns closely with the attendee mix ITWeb reports for the summit.[^11_2][^11_7]
