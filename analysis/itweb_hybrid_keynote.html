The taxonomy used in this keynote

①

Cyber-enabled influence

A cyber operation serves an influence goal: hack-and-leak, symbolic defacement, coerced disclosure, breach claims, stolen data repurposed for narrative effect.

②

Influence-enabled cyber

Deception enables the intrusion: impersonation, fake recruiters, deepfake executives, spoofed journalists, or false pretexts that open the door.

③

Parallel cyber–influence

The same actor runs cyber and influence operations simultaneously, even if the two tracks are operationally independent.

A practical distinction matters throughout: a strict definition requires an unauthorised intrusion; a broader definition includes pure influence operations with no intrusion component.

Under what conditions do these attacks appear?

Election and legitimacy crises

Hack-and-leak, fake polling, manipulated media, and troll amplification spike when actors need to delegitimise institutions, fracture coalitions, or depress turnout.

Hot conflict and wartime mobilisation

Patriotic hacktivism, symbolic defacement, breach claims, and CIB surge when states and proxies seek morale effects, recruitment, or deniable escalation.

Economic distress and low resilience

Double-extortion ransomware, fake jobs, APP fraud, and impersonation campaigns thrive where unemployment, weak controls, and data scarcity make people easy to coerce or deceive.

Information vacuums and trust collapse

Health rumours, xenophobic narratives, climate misinformation, and community incitement spread fastest where official communication is slow, distrusted, or absent.

Global drivers: three engines of hybridisation

🌍

Geopolitical

Multipolar rivalry, wartime contestation, sanctions pressure, and election interference create demand for deniable operations that blur war, diplomacy, and crime.

📉

Economic

RaaS industrialisation, extortion monetisation, stock-price sensitivity, and the low cost of narrative attacks make hybrid campaigns efficient and scalable.

GenAI, deepfake tooling, cheap spoofing, bulletproof hosting, semi-closed messaging channels, and weak provenance standards lower barriers to entry.

Strict definition

When a breach serves the narrative

Double extortion ransomware

Criminal groups no longer just encrypt. They steal, threaten to leak, time disclosures for maximum pressure, and weaponise public shame.

Hack-and-leak

Selective theft and timed release are used to alter political perception, discredit targets, or create strategic ambiguity.

Symbolic defacement

Hacktivists and proxies seek psychological effect more than access persistence: visibility, embarrassment, deterrence theatre.

Deepfake-enabled business intrusion

Synthetic media bypasses trust in identity verification and real-time communications, turning influence into financial breach enablement.

Broader definition

When there is no intrusion — but the harm is real

Coordinated inauthentic behaviour

Fake personas, page networks, bot support, and cross-platform laundering of narratives.

Narrative attacks on brands and elites

Boycott pressure, false claims, executive impersonation, sentiment manipulation, and reputational targeting.

Community-targeted harms

Health rumours, xenophobic incitement, fake jobs, miracle cures, and local-language hate speech.

This broader field is strategically vital because it has been democratised: domestic politicians, contractors, influencers, preachers, scammers, and activists can all play.

The keynote lesson

If your response model is only digital, you will miss the last-mile amplifiers that make information lethal.

South Africa's community harm profile

Xenophobic disinformation

False claims about migrant criminality, jobs, and service access have repeatedly been linked to offline intimidation and violence. Courts have intervened, but the narratives persist.

Faith-mediated health harm

ARV abandonment and miracle-healing claims remain dangerous because they exploit trust, stigma, and economic vulnerability.

Mass scam exposure

Fraud against ordinary households is one of the country's most pervasive information threats and now requires banking-telco fusion responses.

Platform complexity

Real and fake videos circulate simultaneously, making public communication, attribution, and trust restoration much harder during crises.

Response architecture

There is no one-size-fits-all countermeasure

The right response depends on who is being protected and how much information access exists.

The 2×2 response matrix

Elite / institutional protection Ordinary people / community protection
High information access Disinformation security, NI tooling, deepfake detection, court action, platform accountability, independent fact-checking, Scam Signal-style fraud fusion. Media literacy, prebunking, influencer partnerships, rapid reporting hotlines, scam awareness, community verification habits.
Low information access Regional norms, broadcaster accountability, offline journalist support, health-rumour monitoring, protection for fact-checkers and health workers. Faith leader engagement, community radio corrections, oral/local-language literacy, school and clinic outreach, peer-to-peer health and scam resilience.
The most discussed global solutions mostly fit the top-left box. The most life-saving African interventions often sit in the bottom row.

What multilateral and institutional actors are doing

EEAS / EU

Moving from diagnosis to deterrence: FIMI mapping, disruption of enabling infrastructure, DSA enforcement, and content provenance standards.

UNESCO / AU / ECOWAS

Guidelines for information integrity on platforms, model policy work, multilingual resilience approaches, and regional journalist training.

WHO and health actors

Rumour hotlines, infodemic response alliances, and trusted-messenger networks for health emergencies.

Courts and oversight bodies

Where political elites are implicated, independent inquiries, courts, watchdogs, and media freedom protections become part of cyber–influence defence.

What firms should do next

🔐

Treat identity as attack surface

Deepfake detection, adaptive trust, and stronger approval workflows for payments, claims, and identity proofing.

📡

Add narrative intelligence

Know what is building, who is driving it, how it is mutating across languages, and whether it is organic or engineered.

🧩

Build TrustOps

CISO, CCO, legal, marketing, risk, HR, and executive leadership need a single hybrid response playbook.

🗂️

Prepare leak-response playbooks

Assume data theft will be used narratively, not just technically; rehearse disclosure, verification, and stakeholder messaging.

🧾

Use provenance where possible

C2PA-style content credentials and authenticated media pipelines can reduce synthetic confusion in crisis comms.

🤝

Share signals externally

Sectoral intelligence sharing, bank–telco fusion, and CERT coordination are now reputation and safety issues too.

What community-facing actors should do next

Faith leaders: move from being accidental amplifiers to pre-briefed trusted messengers, especially on health and inter-community tension.
Community radio and broadcasters: institutionalise correction segments, local-language verification, and rebuttal rights for harmful rumours.
Schools, clinics, and NGOs: deliver oral and visual media literacy, not just digital literacy, in low-access environments.
Civil society coalitions: document harm, protect fact-checkers and journalists, and create escalation channels with platforms, regulators, and courts.
Banks and mobile operators: fuse scam detection with public education, especially where fraud is routed through messaging apps and social engineering.

The keynote takeaway

In 2026, cyber defence without narrative defence is incomplete — and narrative defence without community trust is fragile.

Protect systems. Protect trust. Protect people.
